TLDR: Could Whipple shields be constructed from some stable or laminar-flowing liquid sheath instead of thin solid layers ("waterfall instead of foil")? ^fe84d0

Regular Whipple Shields use thin layers of solid material to fragment high speed incoming projectiles and thus disperse the force of impact over a larger area of the hull proper. Once a shield has been penetrated at some point, that point needs to be replaced. I wonder whether a sheath of some liquid would also work to fragment the incoming projectile and scatter it across a larger surface. That way, you could replace the liquid lost in the collision from a tank, instead of needing to "re-skin" the Whipple shield. 

Initial idea: A ductile long-polymer gel might be ductile enough to push out and pull back in as a continuous sheath of liquid in 0 G and near absolute 0 temperatures of space. Alternatively, ferromagnetic liquids might be held in place by magnetic fields. 

Insight: Neither of these are energy-neutral. Indeed, most designs will require continuous pumping, so that laminar flow is maintained, or will require magnetic fields to stay in shape. This is not desirable over traditional "foil"-based Whipple shields. 

Solution: A polymer gel that is static at rest but "self-healing" after impact, i.e., that closes holes torn by a projectile by surface tension alone without active pumping except maybe for some replacement of splashed liquid when the liquid sheath has thinned too much.
