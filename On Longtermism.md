#Random_Idea #Philosophy 
The idea of longtermism proposes that our decisions today should primarily be influenced by their long term outcomes. This is based on utilitarianism and the projection that far more people may live in the future than have lived yet. Hence, the argument goes that investing in a better future (even accepting suffering today in extreme examples of longtermism) creates far more overall joy and reduces far more overall suffering, because it affects so many more people. 

Further reading:
https://ourworldindata.org/the-future-is-vast

My thoughts:
This hinges on the ideas that human joy / human absence of suffering is an absolute and not a fractional quantity, i.e., The total amount of suffering or joy is the product of the ```(Avg(individual joy) - Avg(individual suffering) x number of people``` and not just the ```Avg(individual suffering) - Avg(individual suffering)```. In this view, creating life can have a net effect on suffering or joy and creating new life itself becomes part of the utilitarian equation of reasonable estimates can be made how happy that life will be. I can't quite articulate why, but it goes against my intuitive understanding of utilitarianism that life which is not realized yet should factor into the utilitarian equation. It's intuitive to me that we should always strive to avoid suffering for being capable of suffering. However, I don't see an intrinsic value in creating beings capable of feeling joy. In other words, the absence of suffering is a goal dependent on the existence of a creature capable of suffering. In the absence of such a creature, this principle does not extend to the moral obligation of creating a creature capable of feeling joy, as joy itself does not have a meaning unless the creature first exists. If this holds, longtermism falls back to the weak argument of second order protection, i.e., improve the lives for people today as much as you can, unless it infringes on the happiness of future people who will expectantly exist.
