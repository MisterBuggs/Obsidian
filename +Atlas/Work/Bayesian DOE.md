This completely blinds BayBE to the existence of multiple cell lines, thereby increasing noise but baking robustness into one central combined objective.

In phase 1, only cell lines K<sub>1</sub> through K<sub>n</sub> are active. Then, K<sub>1</sub> through K<sub>n</sub> are inactivated, any cell lines that never worked are dropped, and the virtual cell line K<sub>n+1</sub> is activated, which reports a self-calculated composite outcome of performance across cell lines K<sub>1</sub> through K<sub>n-dropouts</sub>, for example by passing the second-worst performance of all tested cell lines to BayBE.

If I understand correctly, passing a [sigmoid transformation](https://emdgroup.github.io/baybe/stable/userguide/transformations.html) before maximization may encode the biological reality that many combinations will not work but some will, i.e, that values of 10 vs 20 vs 30% purity are all equally worthless and values of 90, 93, and 96% are all almost perfect, while values between 70 and 90% may represent gradual improvement worth looking into. This may prevent *hallucinations of gradual improvements* in the low efficiency range, slowing formal optimization but making it count towards actual biological progress. Also see [Composite Transformations](https://emdgroup.github.io/baybe/stable/userguide/transformations#Composite_Transformations.html), e.g., clamping to >40% and then sigmoid with a mid-log point somewhere around 80%.

## Ant Colony Optimization Algorithm for Interpretable Bayesian Classifiers Combination (Bouktif et al., 2014)

This paper is relevant mainly as a *metaheuristic template* for exploring large combinatorial design spaces while maintaining *interpretability* of the learned model. Their key idea is to **decompose models into “chunks”** (here: conditional-probability pieces of Naive Bayes tied to feature-intervals), and then use **Ant Colony Optimization (ACO)** to recombine chunks guided by a context dataset, balancing exploration (pheromone evaporation, stochastic choice) and exploitation (pheromone reinforcement). For your cell-culture DoE setting, what is appropriable is not the Bayesian-classifier specifics but: (i) **chunking** the decision policy/response surface into modular components (e.g., per-factor or per-subspace local models), (ii) an **ACO-style sequential search** that can operate under **noisy outcomes and random failures**, and (iii) a framework for **robustness/generalization across “contexts”** (they use datasets from different populations; you have different cell lines). It does not directly address continuous experimental factors, narrow optima, or dead runs, but it offers a useful paradigm for *guided stochastic search with memory* and a strong emphasis on interpretability of the “final recipe”.

## Adaptive and Safe Bayesian Optimization in High Dimensions via One-Dimensional Subspaces (Kirschner et al., 2019; LINEBO / SAFELINEBO)

This paper is highly relevant to your constraints: **few parallel experiments (batch size ~4), long cycle time, high-dimensional continuous inputs, narrow feasible regions, and dead runs**. The core appropriable idea is to replace full-dimensional BO acquisition optimization with **a sequence of 1D line searches** through the current best point (LINEBO), which makes optimization tractable even in many dimensions and can be aligned with your limited parallelization (each “batch” can correspond to exploring a small number of line subproblems). Critically, by pairing each 1D search with **SAFEOPT**, they obtain a **safe BO** procedure (SAFELINEBO) that is designed to **avoid unsafe evaluations**—directly analogous to avoiding non-viable culture conditions (“dead runs”), provided you can define a safety signal (e.g., viability threshold) and start from a known-safe condition. The paper also targets settings with **invariant/low effective dimension** and provides theoretical/global+local convergence behavior; practically, the method’s “slice plots” resemble interpretable 1D response scans, which can be valuable for biologically noisy systems and debugging failures.

## BOTORCH: A Framework for Efficient Monte-Carlo Bayesian Optimization (Balandat et al., 2020)

This paper is relevant as a *practical implementation backbone* for Bayesian optimization under **noise, constraints, batching, and complex objectives**. The main appropriable concepts are: (i) **Monte Carlo acquisition functions** and **sample average approximation (SAA)**, which let you optimize complex acquisitions with deterministic optimizers by holding MC samples fixed—useful when observations are noisy and expensive; (ii) strong support for **batch (q>1) candidate generation** and asynchronous handling, aligning with your “4 experiments per batch”; (iii) built-in abstractions for **constraints** (including “feasibility-weighted” objectives), which can represent viability/survival constraints and treat dead runs as constraint violations; and (iv) multi-output / composite objectives, which can naturally encode “optimize gene expression while being robust across cell lines” (e.g., objective defined on predicted performance across contexts). BOTORCH itself is not an algorithmic prescription for narrow feasible peaks, but it provides many of the computational tools (constrained BO, noisy EI variants, KG/lookahead acquisitions, parallel candidate selection) you could appropriate once you formalize your experimental objective(s) and constraints.

## Ant Colony Optimization for Learning Bayesian Networks (de Campos et al., 2002)

This earlier ACO paper is relevant as another example of **ACO for structure learning** in a huge combinatorial space under a **decomposable score**, and it offers transferable design patterns: define a **constructive path representation**, a **heuristic gain** for each incremental move, and **pheromone update** to bias future searches. For your optimization, the direct BN-learning goal is not relevant, but the *mechanism* is: incremental construction of candidate solutions using **local gains** (analogous to local improvements in response/feasibility), plus optional **local optimization/hill climbing** after each constructed solution (a hybrid metaheuristic). The paper also emphasizes reusing computations via decomposability—conceptually similar to exploiting **factorized or modular surrogate models** (e.g., additivity, subspace modeling) to reduce experimental burden. Like Bouktif et al., it does not directly handle continuous factors, safety/dead runs, or narrow peaks, but it supports the broader idea of **metaheuristic search with memory** and a **hybrid global+local strategy** suitable when evaluations are expensive and noisy.

## Borrotti (PhD thesis): *An Evolutionary Approach to the Design of Experiments for Combinatorial Optimization with an Application to Enzyme Engineering*

This thesis is directly relevant to your setting because it treats a problem with the same meta-structure: **huge design space, extremely limited experimental budget, noisy measurements, strong interactions, and many “non-working” regions**, and it frames the solution explicitly as **evolutionary / closed-loop experimental design** rather than one-shot classical DoE. The most appropriable ideas are: (1) **closed-loop / sequential batching** (they iterate “generations” of experiments, sized to what the lab can run in parallel—analogous to your 4-per-batch constraint); (2) **hybrid search + surrogate modeling** (MACD: an ACO variant guided by a learned predictive model, then locally refined in simulation) which maps to your need to explore broadly but still exploit narrow peaks efficiently; (3) explicit attention to **constraints/feasibility** (they enforce biological constraints during candidate generation), which is conceptually analogous to avoiding **dead runs** by filtering or penalizing predicted non-viable conditions; (4) recognizing “large p, small n” limits of classical RSM/linear models and moving toward **model-assisted metaheuristics**—useful if your response surface has sharp optima and discontinuous feasibility. A cautionary takeaway that is also useful for you: their attempt to “continuous-ify” a discrete space via MDS + polynomial regression struggled because interactions/ruggedness and small data defeated global response-surface fitting—mirroring the risk that standard RSM will underperform in your noisy, narrow-peak culture landscape. The thesis therefore supports adopting **adaptive, model-guided sequential designs** (with feasibility handling) rather than relying primarily on classical factorial/RSM designs when experiments are scarce and failures are common.

## Batch Bayesian Optimization via Local Penalization (González, Dai, Hennig, Lawrence)

This paper is directly relevant to your “**4 experiments per batch**” constraint: it proposes a practical batch BO heuristic (local penalization) that chooses multiple points per iteration without the heavy computational overhead of full joint batch acquisition optimization. The key appropriable mechanism is **iterative maximize–penalize**: pick the first point by maximizing the acquisition, then **repel** subsequent points away from already-chosen batch points using a *local penalizer* derived from a Lipschitz-style exclusion region. For your cell culture problem—where runs take weeks and you need to maximize information per batch—this supports designing each 4-run batch to be *diverse but still targeted*, rather than wasting parallel runs exploring the same neighborhood. The Lipschitz-based exclusion also aligns with your expectation of **narrow peaks**: it spreads batch points to hedge against missing the peak. It does not itself enforce viability/survival constraints (dead runs), but the penalization mechanism can be layered on top of constrained/safe BO or feasibility models. Another appropriable detail is their **runtime emphasis**: because your bottleneck is wall-clock time per batch, methods that keep non-parallelizable compute cheap are attractive.

## BayBE: a Bayesian Back End for experimental planning in the low-to-no-data regime (Fitzner et al., 2025)

BayBE is relevant as a *practitioner-focused BO platform* for real experimental campaigns under **small data, mixed parameter types, constraints, and operational workflow issues**. Several appropriable features map well to your situation: (i) support for **hybrid spaces** (continuous + categorical) and **mixture parameters** (relevant for media blends, though you’d need to map mixtures carefully); (ii) **transfer learning BO**, which is conceptually aligned with your “robust across different cell lines” goal by allowing reuse of data from related campaigns/contexts; (iii) **automatic stopping** of unpromising campaigns—useful when large regions “don’t work” and you want criteria to stop/redirect rather than continue spending batches; and (iv) emphasis on **encoding choices** for categorical variables, a general lesson for media components, supplements, or protocol choices that may be encoded poorly by one-hot/integers. BayBE is less about a novel algorithm for dead runs, and more about *engineering the BO workflow* so it’s usable in messy real-world experimentation with minimal data.

## Bayesian optimization with adaptive surrogate models for automated experimental design (Lei et al., 2021)

This paper is relevant to your expectation of **non-smooth, rugged response surfaces** and the fact that standard GP surrogates can struggle when the objective has abrupt transitions or only a low-dimensional subset of variables matters. They propose replacing GPs with more flexible Bayesian surrogates—**BART** (Bayesian additive regression trees) and **BMARS** (Bayesian multivariate adaptive regression splines)—within BO. For your cell culture optimization, the appropriable idea is: consider surrogate models that can **handle discontinuities, interactions, and variable selection** more robustly than smooth kernels, especially when data is sparse and failures are frequent. Their results (in materials examples and benchmarks like Rastrigin) support the notion that tree/spline Bayesian surrogates can be more sample-efficient than standard GP-RBF when the surface is “rough” and high-dimensional with many uninformative factors. This is highly compatible with your “narrow peaks + large dead regions” intuition. They don’t focus on explicit safety constraints, but BART-style models can be paired with constraint modeling (viability) in a constrained BO setup.

## Bayesian optimization with known experimental and design constraints for chemistry applications (Hickman et al., 2022; constrained PHOENICS/GRYFFIN)

This paper is strongly relevant to your **dead runs** problem because it addresses **known constraints** in experimental parameter spaces and shows how an optimizer can avoid infeasible regions via constrained acquisition optimization. The key appropriable contribution is an implementation pattern: accept an arbitrary user-defined constraint function \(c(x)\) (feasible/infeasible), and enforce it during acquisition optimization using (a) **rejection sampling + early termination** for gradient-based steps or (b) a **genetic algorithm with projection to the feasibility boundary**. For your cell culture work, if you can encode viability rules (even partial ones) as known constraints—e.g., pH/osmolality bounds, growth factor ranges, gas conditions known to be lethal—this provides a robust way to prevent wasted batches. The paper also highlights that constraints can be nonlinear and interdependent (very plausible for media mixtures and gas settings). While it doesn’t solve *unknown* constraints (where you only learn lethality after running), its approach is a clean foundation for incorporating what you *do* know and reducing the dead-run rate.

## Robust Experimental Design via Generalised Bayesian Inference (Barlas et al.; GBOED / Gibbs EIG)

This paper is relevant to the fact that biological data are **noisy, outlier-prone, and often violate standard likelihood assumptions**, which can cause classic Bayesian experimental design / BOED objectives (e.g., expected information gain under a misspecified noise model) to make poor design choices. The appropriable idea is to replace likelihood-based Bayesian updates with **generalised Bayesian (Gibbs) inference**, i.e., update using a **loss function** designed to be robust (e.g., weighted score matching), and then design experiments using a corresponding **Gibbs expected information gain** criterion. For your case, this is conceptually useful because “random failures” and heavy-tailed noise can act like outliers; robust inference can stabilize model updates so one bad batch does not derail the campaign. It is not a direct recipe for batch-size-4 BO or constraints, but it offers a principled way to make *the inference and design objectives robust to misspecification*, which is a common issue in cell culture where variance changes across conditions and failure modes produce non-Gaussian outcomes.

## A guide to Bayesian optimization in bioprocess engineering (Siska et al.; review/guidelines)

This review is directly relevant as a domain-specific synthesis for BO under **biological variability, batch effects, calibration issues, limited experiments**, and it explicitly discusses extensions you care about: **batch BO**, **multi-fidelity**, **mixed-variable optimization**, **transfer learning**, and **safe BO**. Appropriable takeaways include: (i) strong practical guidance on **initial design** and why excessive replicates can be inefficient in BO (instead model noise and use selective replication for batch effects); (ii) detailed discussion of how **kernel choice and hyperparameter priors** can cause under/overfitting in low-data regimes—important for your narrow-peaked landscape; (iii) explicit emphasis on **batch effects and positional biases**, which are central to robustness across weeks-long batches and across cell lines; and (iv) an overview of how batch BO methods (including local penalization) map to high-throughput/parallel experimentation. While it’s not introducing a new algorithm, it consolidates “what tends to work” in biotechnology settings similar in character to yours and flags where bespoke modeling (e.g., hybrid/physically informed surrogates or batch-effect models) may be necessary.

## Robust Multi-Objective Bayesian Optimization Under Input Noise (Daulton et al.)

Highly relevant because it formalizes *robust* optimization when your setpoints (e.g., O₂/CO₂, growth factors, media ratios) are effectively perturbed at implementation time—matching both biological variability and narrow-peaked “cliff” behavior. The key appropriation is replacing nominal optimization with a *high-probability guarantee* objective via (multivariate) value-at-risk: you can target conditions that meet expression thresholds across perturbations with probability ≥α, rather than chasing brittle optima. Their MARS method (random Chebyshev scalarizations + VAR + noisy EI) is designed for small evaluation budgets and supports batch selection and black-box constraints; your “dead runs” map naturally to constraints and their feasibility-weighting discussion (and risk-level α) gives a principled way to avoid fragile regions.

## Measurements with noise: Bayesian optimization for co-optimizing noise and property discovery (Slautin et al.)

Relevant mainly for your *measurement noise + cost/time* trade-off rather than biological optimization per se. They treat measurement duration as an input and jointly optimize the target property and the measurement noise model, using a separate GP for noise and a reward/cost-aware acquisition. For cell culture, an analog is dynamically choosing replication depth (number of wells/replicates, sequencing depth, imaging time) per condition/batch: allocate more measurement effort where gene-expression uncertainty is decision-limiting, and less where conditions are clearly bad or lethal. While their setup assumes the underlying property is independent of “time” (measurement effort), the core idea—explicitly modeling noise and embedding cost into acquisition—can reduce wasted weeks by adapting assay effort under high failure/noise regimes.

## Optimization for robust engineering design with noise factors (Yingchen & Mei-Han, “EI-GL/UCB-GL”)

This paper connects robust design (Taguchi-like “noise factors”) with Bayesian optimization by optimizing *expected loss* under noise rather than just mean performance, and highlights that quadratic loss is often an unrealistic surrogate—motivating *general bounded loss functions*. For your problem, you can define losses that reflect biological reality: e.g., zero/penalty for cell death (“dead runs”), steep penalties for falling below a gene-expression threshold, or asymmetric losses capturing “must work” regions. Their EI/UCB adaptations for general loss suggest a route to optimize such nonstandard objectives without assuming Gaussianity of the response surface, though the paper appears more engineering-focused and may rely on Monte Carlo/quadrature approximations that you’d need to adapt for sparse, noisy wet-lab data.

## Constrained Bayesian Optimization with Noisy Experiments (Letham et al., NEI)

Directly applicable: your assays are noisy, constraints (viability) are noisy/unknown, and you can run small batches in parallel. NEI derives expected improvement that *integrates over uncertainty in the incumbent best point* (a failure mode of “plug-in EI” under high noise) and extends cleanly to noisy constraints and batch/asynchronous execution via QMC sampling. In your setting, viability constraints and random failures fit naturally, and NEI’s focus on high-noise regimes is a strong match. A practical appropriation is their emphasis that robust performance gains come from properly marginalizing uncertainty (not heuristic EI), which should reduce over-exploitation of spurious “lucky” high-expression runs.

## Learning Relevant Contextual Variables Within Bayesian Optimization (Martinelli et al., SADCBO)

Relevant to “robustness across cell lines” and variable overload: cell line can be treated as a contextual/environment variable (observed or controllable), and SADCBO addresses the case where many contextual factors exist but only some matter. They propose sensitivity-analysis-based variable selection (Feature Collapsing) focused on high-performing regions, plus a two-phase strategy: initially *observe* context without paying to control it, then later *optimize* selected contextual variables if worth the cost. For you, this maps to deciding whether to explicitly include cell line (and other batch covariates) in the model vs treating them as noise, and to selecting which “nuisance” factors (passage number, seeding density, operator, incubator) must be measured/controlled. The phase-switching criterion (stop when improvement saturates) is also relevant under long batch cycles.

## Practical BO with changing environmental conditions (Diessner et al., ENVBO)

This targets optimization when some variables are uncontrollable but measurable each run (temperature, wind)—analogous to batch effects in biology (incubator drift, day-to-day variability, cell state). ENVBO fits a global GP over controllable + environmental variables but *optimizes only controllables conditional on observed environment*, yielding a policy “best settings as a function of environment.” For your project, it suggests framing robustness as learning a mapping from (cell line, batch covariates) → optimal media/gas/growth factor setpoints, rather than forcing one universal optimum. However, it assumes environments are exogenously given per run (not chosen), and it does not directly handle “dead runs” as constraints—those would need added constraint modeling.

## Rationalised experiment design for parameter estimation with sensitivity clustering (Chhajer & Roy, PARSEC + ABC-FAR)

More DoE/identifiability oriented than direct optimization, but still useful if you plan to build mechanistic or semi-mechanistic models of gene expression/viability. PARSEC clusters *parameter sensitivity profiles* over candidate measurement times/conditions to choose informative measurements, and explicitly accounts for parameter uncertainty by concatenating sensitivities across sampled parameter sets. The transferable idea is using *sensitivity-based clustering to pick a minimal, informative set* under constraints (limited runs, limited timepoints), which could help you decide which readouts/timepoints/markers to measure per culture condition or which factor combinations best disambiguate effects. ABC-FAR is a robust likelihood-free estimation approach that may be attractive when noise is non-Gaussian and failures occur, but it is computational and assumes you have a model to simulate.

## Structured sampling strategies in Bayesian optimization (Greif et al.)

Relevant for your tight budgets and narrow optima: initial design choice can dominate early BO performance when only a handful of batches are possible. They benchmark LHS, fractional factorial design (FFD), and hybrids as *initialization* strategies and show that structured sampling can materially change convergence, particularly early. For cell culture, you could use FFD/space-filling to cover main effects/interactions quickly before switching to BO, acknowledging that “edge” sampling may be useful when optima lie near boundaries or feasibility cliffs. This paper doesn’t address constraints/noise deeply beyond standard BO, but it provides evidence and methodology for choosing initial DoE structures under limited evaluations.

## Naïve Bayes Ant Colony Optimization for Experimental Design (Borrotti & Poli, NACO)

Relevant mainly as an alternative for *combinatorial* experimental design (e.g., discrete media components, categorical choices) when the search space is huge and evaluations are few. They combine a Naïve Bayes classifier (to learn which “words”/choices correlate with high performance beyond a threshold) with an ant-colony optimizer that constructs candidates sequentially, biasing exploration toward promising building blocks. In your setting, this could apply to discrete/categorical decisions like selecting among a library of media additives, basal media identities, or discrete growth-factor presence/absence, especially if you binarize outcomes (works/doesn’t work; viable/non-viable; above/below expression threshold). However, the strong conditional-independence assumption and lack of continuous-variable handling would limit direct use; it’s best viewed as a heuristic for discrete mixture/component selection rather than a full solution for continuous gas/concentration tuning.
