#Peer_Review [[2026-04-28]] #ICMx
Reviewer Summary:

Recommendation:
Major Comments:
1. Which reporting guideline was used to prepare this manuscript. Please choose an appropriate reporting guideline, likely from among the various STROBE guidelines, and report your data according to that guidelines. Reporting according to established guidelines is critical for compatibility with later meta-reviews. 
Minor Comments:
2. Title: The term "severe sepsis" should not be used anymore according to the Sepsis-3 guidelines, as it is not well-defined. 
3. Introduction, lines 82-83: The introduction is refreshingly concise. However, in its current form, the research question of this study does not follow naturally from the introduction. The authors broadly introduce miRNA research in sepsis. I would recommend including some prominent examples of well established miRNA-pathology couplings beyond name-dropping certain miRNAs. I would also recommend clearly stating the research question of this study that has not yet been answered by similar previous studies. 
4. Methods, line 94f: "neutropenia", "immunocompromised state", and "other factors that might affect study outcome" are very vague and do not allow reproducing this study. Please provide a clear, actionable list of diagnoses and medications that led to study exclusion. Such a list is commonly found in the supplement of similar studies. 
5. Methods, line 96: Did legally authorized representatives provide *written* informed consent or was verbal informed consent considered sufficient for study inclusion?
6. Methods: Limiting study inclusion to "patients with neurological hemorrhage" may be a legitimate departmental constraint. This constraint to a very specific patient population may limit generalizability and should thus be included in the manuscript title, e.g., "in a cohort of 116 ICU patients with neurological hemorrhage" or similar. Study reporting guidelines (see above) usually also include guidance on informative, discovery-optimized titles. 
7. Methods, line : Sampling in relation to fever spikes is a little outdated but has biological rationale. I commend the authors for stating this so clearly in the abstract. How where sepsis cases without fever handled, which are common especially in the elderly population? I recommend also including documentation how your blood draw timings related to the time of sepsis diagnosis by Sepsis-3 criteria, first antibiotic use, and first collection of blood cultures in a given patient, as opposed to first fever spike. 
8. Methods: How were non-septic controls selected and timed? Were these patients with fever spikes but without SIRS or sepsis? In that case, I highly recommend also including a baseline control of Neuro-ICU patients without signs of infection. 
9. Conclusion: The authors suggest that these markers could be used for early sepsis detection. However, no sampling before fever spikes is being reported. Accordingly, this is a hypothesis that should be clearly worded as an outlook requiring further studies.
Language & Proofing:
10. This may be a function of the editorial manager, but there are currently two different line numberings displayed in the reviewer copy. One of them doesn't match the actual lines, so comments are made using the coherent line numbers.
