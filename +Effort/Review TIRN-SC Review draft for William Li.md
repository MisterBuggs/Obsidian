Wiliam Li had asked me to review his draft for a review paper. I left comments in the manuscript. Here are a few high level critiques.

Living Draft: [Journal of Cellular Signaling Manuscript - Google Docs](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1vcpk4cWa2T9912InaPrbryoeZkd1zO9YwzTPUbKyVww/edit?tab=t.k4b1tlmg3h4p)

High level comments:
- It is not easy to get a paper published without any visual elements, like tables and figures. 
- I'm generally not a huge fan of non-invited narrative reviews, except for perspective papers. Perspective papers require a strong opinion; they are basically the Op-Ed's of academic writing. Systematic reviews, as opposed to narrative ones, are a different beast, gaining legitimacy and scientific value from their structured methodology.
- The paper is pulling on a lot of relevant and interesting threads. However, in its current form, it also meanders between a lot of different qualities of TIRN cells without really comitting to the dip. It's quite jumpy and misses a clear narrative thread. Here are several ideas how to improve this:
	- Consider using the 10-sentence-technique to write a clear backbone first and then stick to it. (Also see https://www.linkedin.com/posts/willem-buys-510087335_writing-a-discussion-for-a-research-paper-share-7452614381336506368-ho6J?utm_source=social_share_send&utm_medium=member_desktop_web&rcm=ACoAAFQxyhwBTuZx6YFTWUwq1sJzBwXVd1L9SY8). Topics should lead into each other. 
	- Trim down the scope to make room for deeper dips into the important topics.
	- Think about a natural order of things before deciding on the actual order of elements. E.g., somatic cell -> totipotent cell or cells in culture -> non-integrated models -> integrated models -> chimeras -> clones. Your flow of information should always follow such trajectories. This can always default to a temporal trajectory, e.g., mESC -> hESC -> miPSC -> hiPSC -> naive protocols 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 -> 2CLC -> hTIRN -> 8CLC -> 4CL -> L4i, etc. 
	- For defining major sections outside of intrduction and conclusion, you can think about the manuscript like a tree (math/CS). Every part of your paper should be a node living on a semantic tree (the manuscript as a whole). Nodes can be lists of related elements (see point above). Subsequent paragraphs can either be subsequent nodes on the same list (along the same branch) or parallel branches. Your major branches inform the manuscript sections. Your elements along on those branches, whether ordered or not, inform the paragraphs. Minor branches may become subsections or paragraphs, as appropriate given max. 3 levels of headings. Alternatively, you can also use an electrical engineering metaphor of different loads in series or in parallel to conceptualize this. You can even draw a mind-map, which has helped me before.
	- Make sure to take the perspective of someone outside the Zambidis lab when introducing topics. 
- Make sure to *very* clearly differentiate between hypothesis and fact in your wording. 
- Initially, it may make sense to include a lot of citations, then trim redundant ones as you refine the manuscript.
