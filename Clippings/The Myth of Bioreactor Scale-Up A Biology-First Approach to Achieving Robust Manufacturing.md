Bioreactor scale-up remains one of the most critical challenges in pharmaceutical manufacturing. Traditionally, the industry has approached this challenge through the lens of engineering equivalence, focusing on matching normalized parameters across development and manufacturing scales. Common strategies include maintaining power per unit volume, impeller tip speed, bulk mixing time, or oxygen transfer coefficient (kLa). Organizations often reinforce these strategies by preserving similar bioreactor aspect ratios or replicating impeller designs, under the belief that this guarantees robust process performance. Despite these best efforts, real-world experience demonstrates that simply matching engineering numbers or equipment geometry does not necessarily result in biologically equivalent processes or consistent operational outcomes.

The essence of effective scale-up lies not in engineering similarity, but in defining the operational space at each bioreactor scale and model to consistently fulfill the biological requirements of the cell culture.

## Engineering Parameters: Descriptors, Not Objectives

Traditional scale-up approaches emphasize maintaining constant parameters such as power per unit volume, impeller tip speed, short bulk mixing times, matching kLa, similar aspect ratios, and comparable impeller types or configurations. These strategies rest on the assumption that hydrodynamic similarity will translate into biological equivalence. However, this assumption weakens as bioreactor size increases. At larger scales, gas–liquid mass transfer mechanisms change, flow regimes evolve, and localized gradients become more pronounced. Even bioreactors with seemingly identical geometry—even within the same equipment family—can display significant differences in oxygen delivery, carbon dioxide (CO₂) removal, and local mixing behavior. Variations across manufacturers and product lines can further magnify these differences.

### A Biology-First View of Scale-Up

Successful bioreactor scale-up is achieved by consistently meeting the cellular requirements under all operating conditions, rather than by simply matching engineering parameters. This biology-first perspective is highlighted through four key considerations:

#### 1\. Oxygen Uptake Rate: More Relevant Than kLa Alone

Although kLa is a valuable measurement, it is insufficient when considered in isolation. The system’s oxygen transfer capability must always be evaluated relative to the culture’s oxygen uptake rate throughout the process, particularly during periods of peak cell density and productivity. Identical kLa values can reflect very different oxygen delivery capabilities, depending on factors such as the proportion of oxygen in the incoming gas and hydrostatic pressure. The central question at larger scales becomes whether the system can reliably meet dynamic cellular oxygen demands under the most challenging operating conditions.

#### 2\. CO₂ Control: A Critical Scale-Up Constraint

CO₂ accumulation is a common, yet often underestimated, risk during scale-up. As bioreactor volume increases, both gas residence times and CO₂ stripping efficiency can change, even if aeration rates seem similar on a volumetric basis. Robust scale-up requires a comprehensive evaluation of CO₂ removal capabilities to ensure that both dissolved and gaseous CO₂ levels remain below inhibition thresholds throughout the culture duration. This consideration is especially important during high-density operations, when metabolic CO₂ generation reaches its peak.

#### 3\. Mixing Relative to Biological Time Scales

Bulk mixing time alone does not adequately indicate scale-up success. The critical consideration is whether mixing occurs faster than oxygen is consumed by the cells. If the oxygen characteristic time is shorter than the effective mixing time, transient oxygen gradients can develop, even if average dissolved oxygen readings seem controlled. Such localized conditions can affect cellular metabolism, productivity, and final product quality. Ensuring oxygen homogeneity requires evaluating mixing performance within the context of biological demand, rather than by arbitrary time targets.

#### 4\. Addressing Localized Heterogeneity at Addition Points

At manufacturing scale, cells are most susceptible not in the bulk volume, but at points where titrants and feeds are introduced. Additions of base, nutrients, and gases can create localized spikes in pH, concentration, or osmolarity if not rapidly diluted and dispersed. Effective scale-up strategies must explicitly consider the placement of addition points, local dilution effects, and flow patterns that minimize persistent gradients. Neglecting localized heterogeneity is a frequent source of scale-dependent variability and process drift.

## Aspect Ratio and Impeller Similarity: Helpful but Insufficient

Maintaining similar aspect ratios or impeller configurations can be helpful in scale-up efforts, but these should be seen as enabling features, not guarantees. Differences in sparger design, gas handling capacity, control strategies, and vessel internals can all significantly impact performance, even when geometry appears comparable. Thus, hardware similarity alone does not replace the need for a scale-specific operational understanding.

#### Key Takeaways

- Bioreactor scale-up cannot be reduced to simply matching engineering numbers or equipment geometry.
- Oxygen uptake rate and CO₂ control are fundamental to achieving biological scalability.
- Mixing performance must be evaluated relative to biological time scales, not just engineering metrics.
- Localized gradients at addition points pose significant risks during scale-up and must be addressed explicitly.
- Operational scale-up strategies should be specifically defined for each scale, bioreactor make, and bioreactor model.

## Conclusion

There is no universal recipe for bioreactor scale-up. Achieving robust and consistent performance requires defining operational capabilities that are appropriate for each scale, grounded in cellular physiology and mass-transfer fundamentals, rather than relying solely on engineering similarity. By guiding scale-up decisions according to the biological needs of the cells, rather than the convenience of matching parameters, processes become more robust, scalable, and manufacturable throughout the product lifecycle.
